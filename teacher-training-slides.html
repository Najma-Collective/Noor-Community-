Teacher Training Guide: Methodology, Processes, and Responsibilities

1.0 Introduction

Core Principles and Tools

Our Model

This guide outlines the essential processes, pedagogical framework, and operational responsibilities for teachers. Our model is built on providing students with flexible, high-quality language instruction, supported by a structured yet adaptable system.

Two Foundational Spreadsheets

At the core of our operations are two foundational spreadsheets that orchestrate student management, scheduling, and curriculum delivery.

The Student Register

This is the primary tool for administrative tasks. Its functions include:

  • Registering student attendance
  • Recording formal assessments
  • Providing access to class WhatsApp group links
  • Providing access to marking criteria

Student Central

This spreadsheet serves as the student-facing portal for all lesson materials. It functions as a dynamic syllabus, displaying the lessons generated by teachers for each week, thereby avoiding complex Google Drive folder navigation.

2.0 Student Organisation

Class Structure

How Students Are Organised

Our student cohort is organised to provide targeted instruction and foster a sense of community.

Geographical Grouping

The entire student body is divided into two principal locations:

  • Gaza
  • West Bank/Other

This separation is primarily for safeguarding and scheduling purposes.

Important Note: Any student not located in Gaza, regardless of their country of residence (e.g., Egypt, Iceland, UK), is placed in the "West Bank/Other" category.

Proficiency Levels

Within each geographical group, students are further organised into five proficiency levels according to the Common European Framework of Reference for Languages (CEFR).

This results in ten distinct level groups:

Gaza

  • A1
  • A2
  • B1
  • B2
  • C1

West Bank/Other

  • A1
  • A2
  • B1
  • B2
  • C1

Teacher Allocation and Student Choice

Multiple teachers are assigned to each of the ten level groups. This provides students with:

  • A variety of teaching styles
  • A variety of class times to choose from each week

Students exercise agency by selecting which classes they wish to attend.

3.0 Teacher Procedures

Responsibilities

Teacher Autonomy

Teacher autonomy and responsibility are key to the smooth functioning of our programme.

3.1 Attendance Tracking

Attendance System

The attendance system is designed for simplicity and efficiency.

Marking Presence

In the Student Register, teachers are only required to mark students who are present in their class for any given week.

Inferring Absence

An absence is automatically recorded by a blank cell next to a student's name for that week.

This removes the need for teachers to actively mark non-attendance.

Intervention Protocol

The administration will monitor attendance records and will reach out to any student who has been absent for two consecutive weeks.

3.2 Scheduling and Timetabling

Schedule Control

Teachers have control over their teaching schedules, which are managed through a dedicated Scheduling Spreadsheet.

Indicating Availability

Teachers must populate the spreadsheet with the times they are committed to teaching.

Critical Point: It is critical that these are the times teachers want to teach, not all potential times they could teach.

The number of entries for a teacher should correspond directly to the number of one-hour classes they intend to deliver.

Scheduling Tiers

The spreadsheet is organised into two tiers to help teachers prioritise their time slots:

Tier 1

Peak student availability

Tier 2

Secondary availability

Positional Integrity

Teachers may enter their name in multiple rows (for different times or days) but must never have duplicate entries in the columns for a single time slot.

Correct

One entry per time slot

Incorrect

Duplicate entries in same column

Visual Timetable

The data from the scheduling sheet is automatically rendered into a user-friendly Visual Timetable for students.

Updating Availability

Teachers are responsible for:

  • Updating their availability on the scheduling spreadsheet
  • Communicating any changes directly to their students via the relevant WhatsApp group
3.3 Safeguarding Policy for Gaza

Gaza Scheduling Protocol

To ensure student safety, a strict scheduling protocol is in place for the Gaza cohort.

The Sunset Rule

Lessons for students in Gaza must never be scheduled within one hour of sunset.

The final class of the day must conclude at least one hour before sunset.

Emphasis: This policy is non-negotiable and is in place to protect students who may need to travel to access an internet connection.
3.4 Communication and Group Access

Onboarding

Upon hiring, teachers receive an email containing:

  • All essential links
  • Their level group assignment

Joining WhatsApp Groups

Teachers must then proactively locate the WhatsApp link for their assigned group in the Student Register and join it.

Important: To minimise notification fatigue, teachers should only join the group(s) to which they are officially assigned.

Sharing Class Links

Teachers will share Google Meet links on the day of the lesson, allowing students to join.

4.0 Syllabus, Lesson Generation

Resource Management

Our Curriculum

Our curriculum is theme-based and generated using a streamlined, AI-assisted workflow.

The Syllabus Structure

The syllabus is located in Student Central.

Structure:

  • Time progresses weekly along the horizontal x-axis
  • Monthly progression down the vertical y-axis

Thematic Modules

Lesson themes are predetermined for each month and CEFR level.

Example: The theme for all A1 students in Month 1 might be "Personal Identity," with specified sub-topics and grammar points.

Example Thematic Module

Level: A1

Month: 1

Theme: Personal Identity

Sub-topics: Introductions, Family, Hobbies

Grammar: Present Simple, Personal Pronouns

Although multiple teachers in a level group use the same source themes, the AI generates unique variations of lessons.

This ensures students receive extensive and varied practice within a consistent thematic framework.

4.2 Resource Delivery and Homework

Lesson Delivery

All classes are delivered on Google Meet, with teachers sharing their screen to present the Google Slides.

Worksheet Generation

Worksheets are considered a vital component of homework.

Process: In the same AI thread used to create the slide deck, teachers can simply request the Custom Gem to "make a PDF worksheet" from the lesson.

Worksheet Storage

Generated worksheets must be uploaded to the designated Google Drive folder for the class.

Purpose: This creates a permanent, accessible repository for all students, including those who could not attend the live session.

5.0 Student Assessment

Framework

Assessment Overview

Regular, structured assessment is crucial for tracking student progress.

5.1 General English (GE) Speaking Assessment

GE Assessment Timing

Assessments are conducted during the final two weeks of each month within dedicated "Speaking Classes."

GE Assessment Procedure

Teachers use the "GE Assessment" tab in the Student Register to record grades for students who attend an assessment class.

GE Marking Criteria

Students are assessed on four key areas:

1
Accuracy
2
Fluency and Coherence
3
Pronunciation
4
Lexical Resource

Feedback Requirement

Teachers must also provide written comments for each student.

5.2 English for Academic Purposes (EAP) Assessment

EAP Assessment Procedure

EAP assessment is recorded on a separate "EAP Assessment Sheet" but follows the same principle of marking students present in the assessment class.

EAP Marking Criteria

The criteria are aligned with the official IELTS framework to provide students with relevant exam practice. Teachers are expected to communicate this benefit to students.

The criteria are:

1
Task Response
2
Grammatical Accuracy
3
Coherence and Cohesion
4
Lexical Resource

General English and EAP Comment Formatting

Feedback Delivery: Teacher comments are automatically mail-merged into a personalised email for the student.

Formatting Rule: To ensure grammatical consistency in the automated email, teacher comments must be written to follow the introductory phrase: "You should focus on..."

Examples:

  • "...your use of complex sentences"
  • "...developing a wider range of academic vocabulary"

6.0 Pedagogical Framework

Lesson Sequence

Our Methodology

Our methodology is grounded in established pedagogical principles to ensure an effective and engaging learning environment.

Lesson Format

All lessons are 60-minute synchronous online sessions delivered via Google Slides in a main online classroom.

Two Primary Modes

At the heart of our lesson design are two primary modes:

1. Standard Mode

2. Speaking Mode

Standard Mode

This mode follows the principles of Task-Based Language Teaching (TBLT).

The lesson is structured around the completion of a meaningful task and follows a clear sequence.

Standard Mode: Lead-in

Purpose: To activate prior knowledge and generate interest in the topic.

Stage 1

Standard Mode: Pre-task Activities

These stages introduce and provide practice with the language needed for the main task.

This includes:

  • Receptive input (reading or listening)
  • Controlled exercises to build confidence with new vocabulary and grammar
Stage 2

Standard Mode: Main Task

A communicative activity where learners use the language they have practiced to achieve a specific outcome, such as problem-solving or decision-making.

Stage 3

Standard Mode: Reflection

A concluding phase for learners to review what they have learned and identify areas for improvement.

Stage 4

Standard Mode: Full Sequence

1
Lead-in
2
Pre-task Activities
3
Main Task
4
Reflection

Speaking Mode

Used primarily for assessment preparation, this mode maximises student speaking time.

Speaking Mode: Progressive Scaffolding

This mode employs Progressive Scaffolding, where a series of semi-controlled speaking activities gradually reduce support, culminating in a final communicative task that simulates a real-world scenario.
