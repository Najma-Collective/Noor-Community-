Pre-task C · Part 2

Model: Language Analysis

Fatima: Okay, so we’ve agreed that the Hebron report is clear: we need a community awareness program. The data showed that the biggest issue was with younger households, so that’s where we need to focus.

Yousef: Exactly. So, for the pilot program, I propose we start with the schools. We could call it the "Future Guardians" program. We would develop workshops for students aged 10 to 14. For me, the key to long-term sustainability is changing the mindset of the next generation.

Fatima: I agree that schools are important. But, my only concern is that it might not create immediate change in the home. The children learn, but do the parents change their habits? An alternative approach could be to focus directly on the households. I'm thinking of a "Household Champions" program.

Yousef: Tell me more. How would that work?

Fatima: We would train local volunteers to conduct friendly home visits. They would offer practical tips and check for simple leaks. The evidence strongly suggests that this kind of peer-to-peer approach builds trust. We could measure success with follow-up surveys and even ask to see their water bills before and after.

Yousef: That’s a good point about building trust. But I wonder if people would find home visits a bit intrusive. With the "Future Guardians" idea, we are invited into the schools. The main advantage is that we can reach a large number of young people efficiently.

Fatima: That's true. Access is easier. But my priority would be ensuring immediate, measurable impact in water consumption. Perhaps we can combine the ideas? We could run the school workshops, and the students' "homework" could be to conduct a simple water audit at home with their parents, guided by our volunteers.

Yousef: A hybrid model... I like that. It's the best of both worlds. We get the long-term educational impact and the immediate household engagement. Okay, let's design that pilot.

Your Task

  1. Identify the phrases Yousef and Fatima use to introduce their ideas.
  2. How does Fatima express her concern about Yousef's idea without being too direct?
  3. What phrases do they use to justify their proposals with reasons?

Highlight or note the phrases you want to reuse later in the lesson.

Task

Designing the Pilot Program

Shift into project planning mode. Apply your analysis to a real-world decision.

Task Context

The Hebron Water Project

The Situation: Your NGO has analyzed the recent Hebron Water Report.

The Key Finding: Water consumption has risen by 15% in five years due to a lack of awareness about water-saving techniques, especially in younger households.

The Goal: Design a 3-month pilot program to test a solution before launching a bigger project.

The Agreed Strategy: A hybrid approach with activities for both schools and households.

Your Role: As the project planning team, decide on the specific details of this pilot program.

Task Instructions · 15 mins

Design the Pilot Program

  1. Discuss and make decisions on the key details below.
  2. Use language from the lesson to propose ideas, justify your choices, and express concerns.
  3. Prepare a 2-minute summary of your pilot program plan to present to the class.

Key Decisions to Make

  • Program Name: What is a catchy, positive name for your program?
  • Target Districts: Which two districts in Hebron will you target for the pilot? Why?
  • Core Activities: One main activity for schools and one for households.
  • Measuring Success: One primary way to evaluate the pilot.
Reporting

Presenting Your Pilot Program

  1. One person from each group shares the name of your program and its main goals.
  2. Explain the most important reason for choosing your core activities.
  3. Describe how you will measure the success of your pilot.

Use sentence stress to highlight the data or outcomes you want the audience to remember.
