```html ESL Lesson Plan: Getting to Know You

Getting to Know You

Level: A1 (Beginner) Duration: 120 minutes Audience: Palestinian Adult Learners (Online)

Main Objective

By the end of this lesson, students will be able to synthesize language for introductions and personal details to meet a new person and exchange contact information in a social or professional context.

Procedure

  1. Ask the students the following questions to start the conversation:

Teacher's Script & Key Language

"When you meet a new person, what is the first thing you say?"

"What is the first question you ask a new person?"

"Imagine you are at a new class or event. What information do you sometimes need to give people?"

Teacher's Tip

The goal here is to warm students up and get them thinking about the two main themes of the lessons (greetings and personal details). Encourage personal answers and don't over-correct errors. Use gestures and simple language to ensure everyone understands.

Focus

Combining vocabulary from both lessons.

Procedure

  1. Model the activity by having a short, simple conversation with a student.
  2. Explain to the class: "First, we say hello (Lesson 1). Then, we ask for information (Lesson 2)."
  3. Put students into pairs in breakout rooms.
  4. Tell them to have a short conversation. They must introduce themselves using "Hello," "My name is...," and "Nice to meet you." Then, they must ask their partner for their nationality and phone number.

Teacher's Script & Key Language

"Hello! My name is [Teacher's Name]. Nice to meet you."

"Hi! I'm [Student's Name]. Nice to meet you too."

"Great! What's your email, [Student's Name]?"

"My email is..."

Teacher's Tip

Listen in on the breakout rooms to see how students are doing. Note down any common mistakes to address later, but don't interrupt their flow. The aim is to get them using the vocabulary in a meaningful context. For A1, the combination of these two simple steps is a key synthesis.

Focus

Using "Subject Pronouns + to be" in the context of "Personal Details".

Procedure

  1. Quickly review the grammar. Write on the shared screen or chat: "I am..." and "My ... is...".
  2. Give an example of how to use this grammar with the new vocabulary.
  3. Put students into small groups.
  4. Give them this task: "Tell your group three things about you. Use 'I am...' or 'My ... is...'."

Teacher's Script & Key Language

"I am from Palestine. My phone number is 059... My email is teacher@email.com."

Students listen and then create their own examples.

(Example student sentence to elicit)

"My name is Fatima. I am from Hebron. My address is An-Naser Street."

Teacher's Tip

This is a fluency-focused activity. Encourage students to speak as much as possible and share their personal information (they can invent details if they prefer). The grammar is a tool for their conversation, not the main focus.

Focus

Using "Wh- questions with to be" in the context of "Making an introduction".

Procedure

  1. Quickly review the grammar by asking the whole class questions.
  2. Give an example of how to use these questions in a friendly conversation.
  3. Put students into pairs.
  4. Set up a role-play: One student is new at a language centre. The other student is a friendly classmate who should introduce themselves and then ask questions to find out the new student's name, nationality, and email address. Then they swap roles.

Teacher's Script & Key Language

"How do I ask for your name?"

"What's your name?"

"How do I ask for your country?"

"Where are you from?"

"Hello, I'm Ahmed. It's nice to meet you. What's your name?"

Students listen to the model conversation.

Teacher's Tip

Encourage students to use "Nice to meet you" to make the role-play more natural and interactive. This activity practises the question forms in a social context rather than a transactional one.

Focus

Integrated practice of all target language.

Procedure

  1. Present this problem to the whole class: "Imagine you are at a community event, like a food festival. You want to make a list of new friends to contact later. What do you need to do?"
  2. Get a few initial ideas from the class, gently guiding them (e.g., "Say hello," "Ask 'What's your name?'", "Ask 'What's your phone number?'").
  3. Put students into small groups.
  4. Tell them to role-play the situation. Each person in the group must introduce themselves to everyone else and get the name and phone number of at least one other person for their "new friends list."

Teacher's Tip

This activity brings everything together. The problem is a realistic context for them to use all the language they have been practicing. Monitor the groups and help with ideas or language if needed.

Focus

Final Role-Play: Tour Company Sign-up

Procedure

  1. Put students into pairs (A and B).
  2. Explain the role-play: Student A works for a local tour company; Student B is a visitor.
  3. Instructions for the role-play: The company representative (A) must greet the visitor (B), introduce themselves, and get the visitor's details for the mailing list (name, nationality, email). The visitor (B) should answer the questions and also ask for the representative's name.

Teacher's Tip

Give students a minute to think about their roles before they start speaking. This task is the main communicative event of the lesson, so give them space to develop the conversation themselves. This scenario combines the professional context of Lesson 1 with the information-gathering of Lesson 2.

Focus

Group Reporting and Discussion

Procedure

  1. Combine two pairs of students to make groups of four.
  2. Tell each student to report back to the new group about their conversation.
  3. After reporting, ask the group a simple question to discuss: "How many different nationalities are in your group's list?"

Teacher's Script & Key Language

(Student A, the tour guide, should report...)

"I met [Student B's name]. He/She is from [Country]. His/Her email is..."

(Student B, the visitor, should report...)

"I met [Student A's name]. He/She works for a tour company."

Teacher's Tip

The reporting stage makes students listen carefully to each other and provides a natural reason to use the target language again. The final question is a simple comprehension check that encourages them to listen to each other's reports.

Teacher's Script & Key Language

"Today, was it easy or difficult to ask for information?"

"What new words do you remember? (name, email, etc.)"

"Can you say 'hello' and ask 'What's your name?' now? (Ask for thumbs up/down)"

Teacher's Tip

This stage encourages students to think about their own learning. Keep it brief and positive, and praise them for their hard work and participation. Using simple questions and gestures like thumbs up/down makes it accessible for A1 learners.
```
