Threads of Memory - Tatreez

Level: B2 Grammar: Passive Voice Duration: 60 Minutes

Part 1: Reading - Museum Exhibition

Read this museum plaque about traditional Palestinian embroidery.

TRADITIONAL THOBE FROM RAMALLAH

Circa 1930 | Donated by the Habash Family

Exhibition Text

This magnificent thobe was embroidered by women in Ramallah during the 1930s. The silk threads were imported from Syria and Egypt. Each motif was carefully chosen to represent the wearer's village and family status.

The "cypress tree" pattern on the chest panel is considered a symbol of resilience. This particular design has been passed down through generations. The red cross-stitch technique is known as "fallahi" style, typical of the central highlands.

Traditional thobes were worn on special occasions such as weddings and harvest celebrations. The garment would be started when a girl was young and would be completed by her wedding day. Sometimes a single dress would be worked on for several years.

After 1948, many embroidery traditions were disrupted by displacement. However, the art has been preserved by diaspora communities. Today, tatreez is being revived by young Palestinians who see it as a form of cultural resistance.

Note: This thobe was restored in 2015 and has been displayed in exhibitions worldwide.

Part 3: Grammar Noticing - Active vs. Passive

Identify whether the following sentences are Active or Passive voice.

1. "This thobe was embroidered by women in Ramallah."
2. "Young Palestinians are reviving tatreez."
3. "The silk threads were imported from Syria."
4. "The art has been preserved by diaspora communities."

Concept Checking Questions (CCQs)

Q1: In passive voice, is the focus on who does the action or what happens?

Answer: What happens (the action/result)

Q2: "The thobe was embroidered." Do we know who embroidered it from this sentence alone?

Answer: No (unless "by..." is added)

Q3: What is the passive formula? (hint: be + past participle)

Answer: form of "be" + past participle (+ by agent)

Part 6: Variant Text - Fashion Blog

Read this blog post about modern Palestinian fashion.

Heritage Meets Modernity: Tatreez on the Runway

Posted by Lina Ayyad | Fashion & Culture Blog

Last week, a stunning collection was unveiled at Ramallah Fashion Week. The designer, Nadia Asad, explained how traditional embroidery is being transformed into contemporary pieces.

"Tatreez has been misunderstood as something old-fashioned," she told me. "But when it is integrated into modern cuts and fabrics, it becomes revolutionary."

Her latest jacket was featured in Vogue Arabia last month. The embroidery was done by artisans in Hebron, and the design was inspired by motifs from the 1920s. "Every stitch is loaded with meaning," Nadia explained.

The collection has been praised by international critics. Orders have been placed from boutiques in Paris, London, and New York. "Palestinian heritage is being recognized globally," she said with pride.

Next season, Nadia's designs will be showcased at Milan Fashion Week. She hopes that through her work, the stories of Palestinian women will be heard around the world.
