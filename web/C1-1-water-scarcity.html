Water Crisis in Palestine: Executive Summary 2024

Palestinian Hydrology Group | Published March 2024

Key Findings

Rarely has the international community witnessed such stark disparities in water access. In the West Bank, Palestinian per capita consumption averages 73 liters per day - well below the WHO minimum of 100 liters. Not only do Israeli settlers consume four times this amount, but they also control access to aquifers that lie beneath Palestinian land.

Under no circumstances should water be weaponized, yet infrastructure is routinely targeted during military operations. In Gaza, the situation is even more dire. Scarcely had the 2014 conflict ended when humanitarian groups warned that 97% of the coastal aquifer was unfit for human consumption.

Never before have we seen such systematic restrictions. Permits required for well-drilling are seldom granted to Palestinians. Only recently did international donors secure funding for a desalination plant in Gaza, yet fuel shortages continue to hamper operations.

Not until water is recognized as a fundamental human right will equitable solutions emerge. Little did communities in the Jordan Valley anticipate that their springs would be diverted to settlements. No sooner had farmers planted crops than irrigation restrictions were imposed.

Recommendations

Only through international pressure can accountability be achieved. Nowhere is the need for climate justice more evident than in Palestine. Hardly ever do mainstream media outlets cover this environmental catastrophe with the urgency it deserves.

Part 5: Variant Text - Op-Ed

The Thirst for Justice

Seldom do I write with such urgency, but the water crisis in Palestine demands immediate attention. Not until we frame this as a human rights violation will policymakers act decisively.

Last summer, I visited a village near Tubas. No sooner had I arrived than residents showed me their empty water tanks. "The trucks come once a week," they explained, "and the prices keep rising." Little did I know that this family pays ten times more per liter than settlers just kilometers away.

Nowhere is the inequality more visible than in the agricultural sector. Farmers in the Jordan Valley - whose ancestors cultivated this land for millennia - can barely irrigate their fields, while newly established settlements boast swimming pools and lush lawns. Rarely does international media cover these juxtapositions.

Not only are communities struggling with scarcity, but they are also dealing with contamination. In some areas, nitrate levels exceed safe limits by 400%. Under no circumstances should children be drinking this water, yet they have no alternative.

Only when the world acknowledges water as a tool of oppression can we begin to dismantle these systems. Never before has climate justice been more intertwined with political justice. Hardly ever do we see such clear-cut cases of environmental discrimination.

The solution requires political will. Not until settlements cease expanding will aquifers be shared equitably. Only by challenging the status quo can we secure water for future generations.
