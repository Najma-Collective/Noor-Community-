Journal of Postcolonial Literature, Vol. 45, 2023

"Silence as Metaphor in Kanafani's Men in the Sun"

Dr. Samira Khalil, Professor of Comparative Literature

Abstract

Ghassan Kanafani's 1963 novella remains a cornerstone of Palestinian resistance literature. If the characters had voiced their suffering, they would be alive today - yet their silence becomes the text's most devastating critique. The three refugees who suffocate in the water tank might have survived if they had knocked on the metal walls.

Critics must have debated this ending for decades. The driver, Abu Khaizuran, asks the haunting question: "Why didn't you knock on the walls?" This can't have been mere negligence. Rather, it must have represented a paralysis born of systematic dehumanization. If Palestinian voices had been heard in 1948, the trajectory would be different now (mixed conditional: past condition, present result).

Historical Context

Kanafani wrote during a period when Palestinian identity was being erased from international discourse. He must have felt an urgency to document these stories. If he were alive today, he would likely be writing about Gaza's siege (hypothetical present/future).

The novella's symbolism is multilayered. The water tank might represent the borders that contain Palestinians, or it could symbolize the silence imposed by power structures. Kanafani can't have chosen this metaphor arbitrarily - every detail was deliberate.

Literary Legacy

If Kanafani had not been assassinated in 1972, he would have continued shaping Palestinian literature for decades. His death must have been a calculated act to silence dissent. Yet his work survives. Contemporary authors might not have had the same courage if Kanafani hadn't paved the way.

The text's power lies in what is unsaid. If readers understand this silence, they will grasp the broader political erasure Palestinians face. It couldn't be clearer: if we had listened then, we wouldn't be witnessing the same patterns now (mixed conditional: past action, present result).

Concept Checking Questions (CCQs)

Q1: What is a mixed conditional? Give the formula.

Answer: Past condition (if + past perfect) → Present result (would/wouldn't + base verb)

Q2: "If he had written more, we would know more now." Is the condition about the past or present?

Answer: Past (but the result affects the present)

Q3: "He must have felt urgency." Does this express certainty or possibility?

Answer: Strong certainty/logical deduction about the past

Read this biographical excerpt about Mahmoud Darwish.

Mahmoud Darwish (1941-2008)

Palestine's National Poet

Mahmoud Darwish must have known from childhood that words would be his weapon. Born in the village of al-Birwa, which was destroyed in 1948, he became a refugee at age seven. If his village had not been demolished, he would be buried there today - instead, he rests in Ramallah (mixed conditional).

His poetry couldn't have been more political or more personal. "Write down! I am Arab," he declared in "Identity Card" (1964). Critics must have recognized immediately that this was more than poetry - it was an assertion of existence. If he had remained silent, Palestinian identity might not have had such a powerful literary voice.

"If I were to start all over again, I would choose the same path - the path of the word, which is more dangerous than the path of the sword."

Darwish lived in exile for decades. If he were able to live freely in Palestine, his work would probably be different, though no less profound (present hypothetical). He might have written about everyday joys rather than loss - but history chose his themes for him.

When he died in 2008, thousands attended his funeral. His impact can't be overstated. If future generations read his work, they will understand that poetry is resistance. He must have believed this deeply - why else would he have risked so much?

Today, Darwish's poems might be the most translated Palestinian literature. If he had known how widely his words would travel, he would probably have been both proud and heartbroken - proud of the art, heartbroken that the struggle continues.
